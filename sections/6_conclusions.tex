\section{Conclusions and outlook}
\label{sec:conclusions}

\paragraph{What was achieved}

Researchers who share video footage online often face an awkward choice. They can redact sensitive regions by hand, adopt commercial tools that solve the full problem at substantial cost, or rely on software that addresses only one part of it. This project aimed to remove that split. The outcome is \texttt{gdpr\_anonymizer}, a public repository that accepts a video file, keeps datasets and weights on the operator's machine, and returns a blurred output through a documented sequence of local commands \cite{RepoGDPR}.

The repository did not begin as an empty codebase. It inherits a Python extractor and a Rust segmentator from upstream tooling \cite{DecrypheExtractor,DecrypheSegmentator}. The contribution described in the preceding chapters is the \texttt{ml\_pipeline} package, which links labelling, training, validation, and blur into one repeatable path. That integration is the central engineering result. Before it existed, even well-labelled frames still depended on external training scripts and improvised anonymisation steps.

Once those stages were connected, the project also addressed a broader accessibility gap. To my knowledge, few open repositories publish the full journey from raw video to mask-based blur for both persons and vehicles. Strong commercial products exist, but they are often closed, costly, or limited to bounding-box redaction. Segmentation masks follow object contours and therefore obscure faces and plates more cleanly than oversized rectangles, which is particularly visible in social-media footage. Early trials on short clips with a modest label set already produced convincing blur, which suggests that richer annotation should strengthen a reusable checkpoint without redesigning the workflow.

\paragraph{Validation in practice}

Design completeness, however, is not the same as practical reliability. The decisive question was therefore whether the pipeline would behave acceptably on real footage. It did, and more consistently than initially expected. Small labelled samples were enough to reach usable validation quality, and blur remained acceptable on clips of several thousand frames.

Those results do not imply that quality has reached a ceiling. Accuracy still depends on label diversity and polygon precision, as in any supervised segmentation project. What changed is the cost of improvement. Warm-started iteration and error analysis allow the operator to refine labels and retrain without discarding earlier progress. Execution also remained local throughout the evaluation. A GPU shortened training and blur runtime, but it was never a prerequisite: the full pipeline ran on a conventional workstation and still delivered usable validation scores and convincing blur on long clips, which is remarkable for an end-to-end video anonymisation workflow.

\paragraph{Outlook}

The remaining limitations follow directly from that success. The most visible is temporal flicker. When masks shift abruptly between neighbouring frames, blur appears unstable even though each frame is handled correctly in isolation. A natural extension is to propagate stable segment geometry across short frame gaps before applying the existing blur stage. That change would preserve the current per-frame architecture rather than replace it.

Model quality will likewise continue to reflect annotation effort. Sharper polygons on difficult faces and plates, together with broader training material across pedestrians, plate styles, and countries, will matter more than any isolated code change. Assisted region selection in the segmentator could reduce the manual burden and improve boundary precision. Each of these directions builds on the foundation already in place.

The project began with a simple requirement: sensitive video should be shareable without sending private data elsewhere. It ends with a local path from ingest to anonymised output and with evidence that modest labels can already produce useful blur. \texttt{gdpr\_anonymizer} is offered openly so that others can extend that path when their footage, and their responsibility, demand the same care.
